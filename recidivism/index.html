Public Safety & Policy

Gun Violence Is
a Design Problem

The United States has the highest rate of community gun violence of any developed democracy. We've spent decades arguing about people: the shooters, the victims, the lawmakers. The argument has produced almost nothing. The design argument might.

Start with two states. Mississippi has a gun death rate of 29.4 per 100,000 people. Massachusetts has a gun death rate of 3.7 per 100,000. Someone living in Mississippi is eight times more likely to die from gun violence than someone living in Massachusetts. That gap has been consistent for years, and it sits at the center of every serious debate about gun policy in America.

The standard explanation is gun laws. Massachusetts has comprehensive licensing requirements, universal background checks, and an extreme risk protection law. Mississippi has done the opposite: in 2016, the state passed constitutional carry legislation, eliminating the permit requirement for concealed handguns entirely. No permit, no background check for private sales, no waiting period, no registration, no assault weapons ban. Mississippi did not just have weak gun laws. It legislatively codified the elimination of structural oversight as a policy position.

But here is where the design argument becomes more interesting, and more uncomfortable, than either side of the gun debate typically allows. Gun laws explain part of the gap between these two states. They do not explain all of it. Mississippi and Massachusetts also differ sharply in their poverty rates, their investment in community infrastructure, their housing stability, and the health of their social institutions. The structural conditions that produce violence run deeper than the laws that govern firearm access.

The violence is clustered where the structural failures are deepest.

Why the Debate Goes Nowhere

The American gun violence debate has two primary positions, and they share a common flaw: both locate the problem in people rather than systems. The conservative argument focuses on individual moral failure: mental illness, family breakdown, personal responsibility. The progressive argument focuses on individual access: background checks, magazine limits, waiting periods. Both assume that if you change the person or change what the person can access, the outcome will change.

There is evidence that access restrictions work. The Massachusetts-Mississippi comparison makes that clear. But the 5% of city blocks finding points to something the access debate tends to miss. About half of all gun violence in American cities can be traced to neighborhoods shaped by decades of structural decisions: redlining policies that concentrated poverty and severed intergenerational wealth, disinvestment in schools and infrastructure, the removal of economic opportunity, and the systematic underfunding of the community institutions that interrupt cycles of violence. The geography of gun violence is, almost precisely, the geography of structural neglect.

In 2024, the U.S. Surgeon General declared gun violence a public health crisis. 2023 recorded the third-highest number of gun-related deaths ever reported in the United States. Black Americans face gun homicide rates 13 times higher than their white counterparts. These disparities are not explained by individual behavior. They are explained by the systems those individuals inhabit.

What Works and Why

The interventions with the strongest evidence base share a common feature: they treat gun violence as a community design problem, not an individual character problem. Community Violence Intervention programs deploy credible messengers, often formerly incarcerated people, to interrupt conflicts and connect high-risk individuals to services. These programs have produced measurable reductions in shootings in cities including Chicago, Oakland, and Baltimore.

Los Angeles's Gang Reduction and Youth Development program took a structural approach: investing in youth employment, mental health services, community centers, and conflict mediation in the 22 neighborhoods with the highest violence rates. Even after COVID-era increases, Los Angeles homicides remain more than 20% below their pre-program average. The program did not change the people. It changed what the neighborhoods were built to offer them.

A 2017 review of community programs across 264 cities found that for every 10 additional nonprofit crime-reduction programs per 100,000 residents, homicides fell by 9% and violent crime fell by 6%. The structural variable, community institutional density, predicted violence reduction better than policing strategy or gun law stringency. Communities with more institutional resources to absorb conflict produce less violence. That is a design finding.

Design Failure

Decades of "tough on crime" policing concentrated enforcement in the same 5% of city blocks without addressing the structural conditions that produced the violence. Crime would fall during intensive policing periods, then return. The underlying architecture of concentrated poverty, absent economic opportunity, and depleted community institutions regenerated the conditions for violence as reliably as a garden produces weeds when you pull them without changing the soil.

Design Success

Hospital-based Violence Intervention Programs identify victims of gun violence in the ER at the moment of highest receptivity and connect them to case management, employment support, and behavioral health services before discharge. The strongest risk factor for gun violence is prior violent injury; HVIPs interrupt that cycle at the structural level. Cities with mature HVIP programs have seen injury recidivism rates drop significantly from the baseline of 45% within five years.

The Adaptation Problem

One reason gun violence is so resistant to intervention is that the systems producing it are themselves adaptive. Communities experiencing concentrated violence develop informal structures: social norms around retaliation, economic activity built around illegal markets, distrust of institutions. These serve real functions within that environment. They are not irrational responses. They are rational adaptations to a structural reality where legitimate economic opportunity is scarce, institutional trust has been repeatedly violated, and physical safety depends on social reputation.

This is why interventions that change behavior without changing structure tend not to hold. You can counsel a 19-year-old out of a gang, but if the neighborhood he returns to offers no employment, no safety, and no alternative social network, the structural pressure that drove him into the gang is still operating. The system adapted him. A different system will produce a different adaptation.

The communities making the most sustained progress against gun violence, including Cure Violence sites, cities with mature community violence intervention programs, and neighborhoods with strong nonprofit density, have one thing in common: they changed what the community was built to offer. They did not just remove the bad options. They built the good ones.

The Design Question

"If we mapped the structural conditions of every high-violence neighborhood in America, the poverty rates, the school funding, the institutional density, the economic opportunity, what would we find that the violence debate has been missing?"

Gun violence in America is not a mystery. It is a map. It follows the contours of structural disinvestment with remarkable precision. The argument about guns and people has produced decades of stalemate. The argument about design, about what these communities were built to offer and what we could build instead, is just beginning. It is a more honest conversation. And it may be the only one with a real answer at the end of it.
